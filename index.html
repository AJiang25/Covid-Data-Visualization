The Big Picture

What does COVID-19 actually look like
from inside the body?

Most COVID-19 data tells you who died — older patients, those with comorbidities, certain zip codes. This dashboard asks a different question: how did they die, and what physiological signals separated those who recovered from those who didn't?

Using clinical records from 9,106 COVID-19 patients in Massachusetts (January–April 2020), we trace the disease's path through the body — day by day, complication by complication — to build a complete picture of COVID-19's progression from first diagnosis to death or recovery.

The Four Acts
📈
ACT 1 Vital Trajectories

From the moment of diagnosis, two paths emerge. We track four clinical signals — body temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, and lymphocyte count — day by day for 21 days. By day 7, survivors are visibly recovering. Non-survivors are not. The story is the divergence: the fever that never breaks, the heart that won't slow down, the lungs that keep working harder, and the immune cells that were already depleted before the first symptom appeared. A vertical marker shows the median day of death (day 12), anchoring the biological story in time.

⛓
ACT 2 Complication Cascade

COVID-19 doesn't kill randomly — it follows a predictable sequence of escalating complications. Act 2 maps that cascade: cough and fever give way to pneumonia and hypoxemia, which lead to respiratory failure, then sepsis, then ARDS, then septic shock. The key insight is in the branching points: pneumonia appeared in nearly all fatal and non-fatal cases — it is common and survivable. Sepsis appeared in 97% of deaths but only 4% of survivors. ARDS appeared in 63% of deaths and essentially 0% of survivors. These are the thresholds that separate recovery from death. A histogram of days-to-death shows the second week (days 7–14) is when most deaths occur — connecting directly back to the vital sign divergence in Act 1.

🏥
ACT 3 Disease Severity

Zooming out from the individual body to the population level, Act 3 shows that COVID-19 is a bifurcated disease. 78% of patients managed it entirely as outpatients with near-zero mortality. But once the body could no longer manage the disease at home — the 30% who required hospitalization — mortality jumped to 12.8%. The disease does not exist on a spectrum; there is a cliff. A second chart shows that age is the single strongest predictor of who falls off that cliff: patients 75 and older faced a 21.4% mortality rate — 27 times higher than those aged 30–44.

📅
ACT 4 Epidemic Timeline

Act 4 pulls back to the broadest view: the epidemic curve itself. A dual-axis chart overlays weekly new cases (bars) with weekly deaths (line) from January through April 2020. The defining feature is the lag — the death peak follows the case peak by almost exactly two weeks. This is not a coincidence. It is the direct signature of the 12-day median survival window measured in Act 2, playing out at population scale. The virus has a predictable clock, and Acts 1 through 3 explain exactly what is happening during those 12 days.

A predictable descent — one complication at a time

COVID-19 follows a cascade: fever and cough → pneumonia → respiratory failure → sepsis → ARDS → shock → death. Almost every patient who died passed through each stage in order.

ACT 3 / 4

Mild for most — catastrophic for those hospitalized

78% of COVID patients managed outpatient had near-zero mortality. Of those hospitalized, 12.8% died. The disease is bifurcated: mostly mild, but lethal once it escalates past the outpatient threshold.

Mortality by Care Setting
The cliff between outpatient and hospitalized
Hospitalized: 12.8% mortality. ER and urgent care: near-zero. Crossing the threshold into inpatient care — meaning the body could not manage the disease at home — dramatically changes the outcome.
Mortality by Age Group
COVID-19's lethality is exponential with age
Patients 75+ have a 21.4% mortality rate — roughly 27× higher than those aged 30–44 (0.8%). Below 45, mortality stays under 1%. The jump from 60–74 (8.7%) to 75+ (21.4%) is the steepest cliff in the entire dataset. Age is the single strongest predictor of death.
